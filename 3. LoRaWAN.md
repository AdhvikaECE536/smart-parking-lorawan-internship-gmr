LoRa is not like Wi-Fi or Bluetooth. It does not send fast, narrow bursts. It sends a chirp: a signal that sweeps continuously across a range of frequencies.

### Why a chirp?
Because it is incredibly robust against noise and interference. Even if part of the chirp is drowned out by another radio or electrical noise, the receiver can still reconstruct the data from the surviving fragments. It is like shouting a song slowly in a noisy room instead of whispering quickly.

Spreading Factor (SF) controls the speed:

| SF   | Chirp Speed               | Range | Battery Cost |
| ---- | ------------------------- | ----- | ------------ |
| SF7  | Fast (~5.5 ms per symbol) | Short | Low          |
| SF12 | Slow (~820 ms per symbol) | Long  | High         |

We dont always use SF12. A single SF12 packet can take over a second of airtime. That burns battery and eats into the legal duty-cycle limit (we cannot occupy the channel more than a small percentage of time). The network uses ADR (Adaptive Data Rate) to push the sensor to the fastest SF it can reliably use.

A few hundred meters away, a LoRaWAN Gateway is put with its radio receiver constantly scanning multiple channels and spreading factors simultaneously.
It has 8 or more parallel radio channels. Each sensor chirps briefly and then goes silent. The probability of two sensors chirping at the exact same microsecond on the exact same channel is low. If collisions happen, the sensors retry later (randomised back-off). This helps in using one gateway hear many sensors.

A gateway demodulates the chirp, recovers the raw bytes, wraps them in a UDP/IP packet (Semtech Packet Forwarder protocol), and sends them over Ethernet or 4G to the ChirpStack server. The gateway does not decrypt, does not interpret, and does not care about parking. It is a pure radio-to-IP bridge.

### 2 Directions

| Direction        | Name         | Parking Example                                    | Why it matters                                                                |
| ---------------- | ------------ | -------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sensor → Gateway | **Uplink**   | "Bay xyz is occupied (1)"                          | The main business data. Carried by the chirp we just followed.                |
| Gateway → Sensor | **Downlink** | "I got your packet (ACK)" or "Change your SF to 9" | Used sparingly. The sensor must wake its receiver to listen, burning battery. |

After every confirmed uplink, the sensor opens a receive window (RX1 at 1 s, RX2 at 2 s). During those windows, the radio is in receive mode, drawing ~10 mA. If the sensor sends 20 confirmed packets a day, that is 40 seconds of receive time. Over years, that adds up. So downlinks are reserved for ACKs and critical MAC commands only.

---
### Confirmed and Unconfirmed uplinks

**Confirmed Uplink**
- The sensor sends data and demands an ACK.
- The gateway must schedule a downlink ACK within the RX1/RX2 windows.
- ChirpStack coordinates this so multiple gateways do not all try to ACK at once.
- **Why use it?** When the bay state flips (0→1 or 1→0). The business logic depends on accurate occupancy. A lost packet means a car is invisible to the system.

**Unconfirmed Uplink**
- The sensor sends data and ignores the reply.
- No RX window needed. The radio shuts off immediately after TX.
- **Why use it?** For periodic heartbeats ("I am alive, battery at 80%"). If one heartbeat is lost, the next one arrives in a few hours. The cost of a missed heartbeat is low; the battery savings are high.

### Security

The packet is just radio waves. Anyone with a cheap Software-Defined Radio (SDR) can capture it. But LoRaWAN has two layers of AES-128 encryption:
1. NwkSKey (Network Session Key): Used to compute the MIC (Message Integrity Code). The gateway and ChirpStack share this. It proves the packet was not tampered with and prevents replay attacks (old packets cannot be re-injected because the frame counter must always increase).
2. AppSKey (Application Session Key): End-to-end payload encryption. Only the sensor and the application owner (GMR) hold this key. Even the gateway operator cannot read whether the bit is 1 or 0.

**Why two keys?**

It separates infrastructure trust from data trust. The network operator (who owns the gateway) can verify packet integrity and manage the network, but they cannot eavesdrop on parking data. The application owner controls the business secrets.

**Why a frame counter?**

Without it, an attacker could record a valid packet and replay it later. ChirpStack rejects any packet whose counter is not higher than the last seen value. This is a simple but effective anti-replay mechanism.

### Duty Cycle

In India's unlicensed 865 MHz band, regulations say you cannot transmit more than a certain percentage of time per channel (typically 1%).

If the sensor used SF12 and sent a 10-byte payload every minute, it would violate the law and interfere with other users. By keeping the payload to 1 bit and using ADR to drop to SF9 or SF7, the sensor stays well within legal limits and is a good citizen of the shared spectrum.
